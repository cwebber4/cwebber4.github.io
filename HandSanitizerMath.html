Introduction

Alcohol-based hand sanitizer in its simplest form is a mix of 2 ingredients: rubbing alcohol (isopropyl alcohol) and aloe vera gel. The rubbing alcohol provides the means by which to kill germs, while the gel gives the mixture a more solid consistency. In order to be effective, the CDC recommends that hand sanitizers contain an alcohol content of at least 60%.

One common misunderstanding is that by using 60% rubbing alcohol, the mixture of rubbing alcohol and gel will thus be 60% alcohol. To the contrary, adding gel to rubbing alcohol will only further dilute the content of alcohol in the resulting mixture. It is no different than adding in more water to the rubbing alcohol – more non-alcoholic substances dilute the alcohol concentration.

In order to ensure that hand sanitizer contains an alcohol content of 60%, the strength of the rubbing alcohol, the amount of rubbing alcohol, and the amount of aloe vera gel must all be correctly proportional to one another. In this article, the mathematical relationships of the proportion of rubbing alcohol to gel in hand sanitizer will be explored. A few formulas will be proposed that may describe these relationships, and examples of their usage will be given. An intermediate to advanced level of mathematics is assumed.

Deriving the Formulas

A few variables must be defined in order to describe mathematically the proportion of rubbing alcohol to gel in hand sanitizer:

Variable Description
R The volume of rubbing alcohol.
G The volume of gel.
W The percentage of water in the rubbing alcohol.
I The percentage of alcohol in the rubbing alcohol.
A The percentage of alcohol in the mixture of rubbing alcohol and gel.


With these variables, some basic mathematical relationships between them can be stated:

Relation Description
W=1−IW = 1 \minus I The percentage of water in the rubbing alcohol.
WR WR The total volume of water in the rubbing alcohol.
G+WR G \plus WR The total volume of non-alcohol in the mixture of rubbing alcohol and gel.
G+R G \plus R The total volume of the mixture of rubbing alcohol and gel.
G+WRG+R \frac{G \plus WR} {G \plus R} The percentage of non-alcohol in the mixture of rubbing alcohol and gel.


Given that the percentage of alcohol and the percentage of non-alcohol in the hand sanitizer mixture must add up to 100%, the total mixture can be described by this formula:

A+G+WRG+R=1 A + \frac{G \plus WR}{G \plus R} = 1

From here, simple algebra can be used to solve for the total percentage of alcohol in the hand sanitizer, the required volume of gel, and the required volume of rubbing alcohol:

A=1−G+WRG+R A = 1 \minus \frac{G \plus WR}{G \plus R}

G=R(1−W−A)A G = \frac{R(1 \minus W \minus A)}{A}

R=AG1−W−A R = \frac{AG}{1 - W - A}

Examples

Some examples will now be presented to demonstrate the usage of these formulas. To start, assume that 70% rubbing alcohol is to be mixed with 4 cups of aloe vera gel. How much rubbing alcohol is needed in order to produce hand sanitizer with a 60% alcohol concentration? Solving for R will yield the amount:

G=4cups G = 4\ cups A=.6 A = .6 I=.7 I = .7 W=1−.7=.3 W = 1 \minus .7 = .3

R=AG1−W−A R = \frac{AG}{1 - W - A}

R=(.6)(4cups)1−.3−.6 R = \frac{(.6)(4\ cups)}{1 - .3 - .6}

R=2.4cups.1 R = \frac{2.4\ cups}{.1}

R=24cups R = 24\ cups

This seems like a lot of rubbing alcohol, but much rubbing alcohol is required in order to maintain a 60% concentration of alcohol alongside the gel and water content. The example can be modified to demonstrate this. Assume now that there are 24 cups of 70% rubbing alcohol and 4 cups of aloe vera gel. The alcohol percentage of the resulting hand sanitizer mix can be discovered by solving for A:

G=4cups G = 4\ cups R=24cups R = 24\ cups I=.7 I = .7 W=1−.7=.3 W = 1 \minus .7 = .3

A=1−G+WRG+R A = 1 \minus \frac{G \plus WR}{G \plus R}

A=1−4cups+(.3)(24cups)4cups+24cups A = 1 \minus \frac{4\ cups \plus (.3)(24\ cups)}{4\ cups \plus 24\ cups}

A=1−11.2cups28cups A = 1 \minus \frac{11.2\ cups}{28\ cups}

A=1−.4 A = 1 \minus .4

A=.6 A = .6

Similarly, if there are 24 cups of 70% rubbing alcohol, and a 60% alcohol concentration is desired, the amount of gel necessary is derived by solving for G:

R=24cups R = 24\ cups I=.7 I = .7 W=1−.7=.3 W = 1 \minus .7 = .3

G=R(1−W−A)A G = \frac{R(1 \minus W \minus A)}{A}

G=(24cups)(1−.3−.6).6 G = \frac{(24\ cups)(1 \minus .3 \minus .6)}{.6}

G=(24cups)(.1).6 G = \frac{(24\ cups)(.1)}{.6}

G=2.4cups.6 G = \frac{2.4\ cups}{.6}

G=4cups G = 4\ cups

Of course, not all combinations of ingredients are possible. If a certain combination is not possible, the resulting answer will either be negative or the formula will produce a division by zero. For example, if 2 cups of gel are added to 60% rubbing alcohol, and a 60% alcohol concentration is to be maintained, a division by zero will be produced.

G=2cups G = 2\ cups A=.6 A = .6 I=.6 I = .6 W=1−.6=.4 W = 1 \minus .6 = .4

R=AG1−W−A R = \frac{AG}{1 - W - A} R=(.6)(2cups)1−.4−.6 R = \frac{(.6)(2\ cups)}{1 - .4 - .6} R=(.6)(2cups)0 R = \frac{(.6)(2\ cups)}{0}

This means that the scenario is unsolvable. Physically, there is no amount of gel that can ever be added to 60% rubbing alcohol in order to keep an alcohol concentration of 60%. Mathematically, the solution space for yielding a 60% concentration of alcohol using 60% rubbing alcohol is any amount of that rubbing alcohol used alone (i.e. infinite solutions).

Likewise, nonsensical answers will be produced from nonsensical starting values. For instance, hand sanitizer with an 80% alcohol concentration is impossible when using only 70% rubbing alcohol. Assuming 2 cups of gel are used, the amount of rubbing alcohol that the formula for R yields is physically incoherent:

G=2cups G = 2\ cups A=.8 A = .8 I=.7 I = .7 W=1−.7=.3 W = 1 \minus .7 = .3

R=AG1−W−A R = \frac{AG}{1 - W - A}

R=(.8)(2cups)1−.3−.8 R = \frac{(.8)(2\ cups)}{1 - .3 - .8}

R=1.6cups−.1 R = \frac{1.6\ cups}{-.1}

R=−16cups R = -16\ cups
